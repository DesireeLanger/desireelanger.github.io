• Pyrrolysyl tRNA synthetase (PylRS) is an enzyme which attaches pyrrolysine - the 22nd genetically encoded amino acid - to its cognate tRNA:

    • Pyl + tRNAPyl + ATP → Pyl-tRNAPyl + AMP + PPi
    Pyrrolysine is encoded by the stop codon UAG in some archaea and bacteria (Srinivasan et al. 2002). This is achieved through a specialised molecule tRNAPyl which recognises the UAG stop codon.

    This domain architecture differs between archaea and bacteria (Srinivasan et al. 2002). The archael PylRS contains two domains - an N-terminal domain involved in tRNA recognition, and a C-terminal catalytic domain, which is also involved in tRNA binding. The full-length archael PylRS was found to be quite insoluble, and hence the structures of the domains have been solved in isolation (Suzuki et al. 2017). However, the bacterial PylRS is encoded by two genes - pylSn (encoding the N-terminal domain), and pylCn (encoding the C-terminal domain). The latter protein is dimeric (Lee et al. 2008), but the full bacterial enzyme has not been studied as extensively as the archaeal enzyme, and likely exists as a heterotetramer.

    The catalytic domain of PylRS is similar to that of SepRS and the five PheRS familes. Collectivey, these members constitute subclass IIc (Kavran et al. 2007, Perona et al. 2012, Valencia-Sánchez et al. 2016, Gomez et al. 2020). The catalytic domain itself is quite typical among class II, and contains six antiparallel strands. Like most members of the superfamily, ATP binding is coordinated by the arginine tweezers, located in motifs 2 and 3 (Kaiser et al. 2018).

    The N-terminal domain binds the T-loop and variable loop of tRNAPyl (Suzuki et al. 2017), in contrast with many amino acyl tRNA synthetases which instead recognise the anticodon. It appears that PylRS lacks editing activity (Gomez et al. 2020).

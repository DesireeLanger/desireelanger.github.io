• Pyrrolysyl-tRNA synthetase (PylRS) is an enzyme that plays a crucial role in protein synthesis by catalysing the attachment of the amino acid pyrrolysine to its cognate tRNA:
    $ \text{Pyl} + \text{tRNA}^\text{Pyl} + \text{ATP} \xrightarrow{\text{PylRS}} \text{Pyl-tRNA}^\text{Pyl} + \text{AMP} + \text{PP}_i $
    Pyrrolysine is encoded by the stop codon UAG in some archaea and bacteria (Srinivasan et al. 2002), which is achieved through a specialized molecule tRNAPyl that recognizes the UAG stop codon. The domain architecture of PylRS differs between archaea and bacteria (Srinivasan et al. 2002). The archaeal PylRS contains two domains: an N-terminal domain involved in tRNA recognition, and a C-terminal catalytic domain, which is also involved in tRNA binding. The full-length archaeal PylRS was found to be quite insoluble (Suzuki et al. 2017). In contrast, the bacterial PylRS is encoded by two genes, pylSn (encoding the N-terminal domain), and pylCn (encoding the C-terminal domain). The latter protein is dimeric (Lee et al. 2008), but the full bacterial enzyme has not been studied as extensively as the archaeal enzyme, and likely exists as a heterotetramer.

    The catalytic domain of PylRS is similar to that of SepRS and the five PheRS families, which collectively constitute subclass IIc (Kavran et al. 2007, Perona et al. 2012, Valencia-Sánchez et al. 2016, Gomez et al. 2020). The catalytic domain is typical among class II enzymes, containing six antiparallel strands. The binding of ATP is coordinated by the arginine tweezers, located in motifs 2 and 3 (Kaiser et al. 2018). The N-terminal domain binds the T-loop and variable loop of tRNAPyl (Suzuki et al. 2017) in contrast with many amino acyl tRNA synthetases which instead recognize the anticodon. It appears that PylRS lacks editing activity (Gomez et al. 2020).

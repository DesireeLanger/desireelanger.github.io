• Asparaginyl tRNA synthetase (AsnRS) is an enzyme which attaches asparagine to its cognate tRNA:
    • Asn + tRNAAsn + ATP → Asn-tRNAAsn + AMP + PPi
    Some organisms lack AsnRS, in which case their incorporation into the polypeptide can be achieved using a non-discriminating AspRS. A parallel mechanism has been observed in the class I GluRS and GlnRS families, leading to the hypothesis that AsnRS and GlnRS were among the last to appear (de Pouplana and Schimmel 2001).

    The three dimensional structure of AsnRS closely resembles that of AspRS and LysRS, with an N-terminal anticodon binding domain and a C-terminal catalytic domain. These three members constitute subclass IIb (Cusack et al. 1991, Valencia-Sánchez et al. 2016, Gomez et al. 2020). The class IIb synthetases of many eukaryotes contain a flexible domain at their N-terminii which help to anchor the synthetase onto the tRNA (Frugier et al. 2000).

    The C-terminal catalytic domain of AsnRS is quite typical of a class II aaRS, however the catalytic domain contains six antiparallel strands rather than seven (same as AspRS). Like most members of the superfamily, ATP binding is coordinated by the arginine tweezers, located in motifs 2 and 3 (Kaiser et al. 2018). In the active site, asparagine recognition is largely achieved through water molecule interactions, in contrast with AspRS where aspartate recognition is largely achieved through direct interaction with the protein (Iwasaki et al. 2006). This specificity is also achieved by the replacement of positively charged residues with negatively charged ones in the active site (Kern et al. 2005).

    It appears that AsnRS lacks editing activity (Gomez et al. 2020).

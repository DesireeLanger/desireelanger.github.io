• The bacterial-like phenylalanyl-tRNA synthetase (PheRS-B) is an enzyme that plays a crucial role in protein synthesis by catalysing the attachment of the amino acid phenylalanine to its cognate tRNA:
    $ \text{Phe} + \text{tRNA}^\text{Phe} + \text{ATP} \xrightarrow{\text{PheRS-B}} \text{Phe-tRNA}^\text{Phe} + \text{AMP} + \text{PP}_i $
    The phenylalanyl-tRNA synthetases (PheRS) are considered to be one of the most evolutionarily complex enzymes among aminoacyl-tRNA synthetases (Klipcan et al. 2010). Unlike most members of the class II superfamily, which are homodimeric, PheRS-B operates as a heterotetramer composed of two α and two β subunits α2β2. There are three known variants of PheRS: one found predominantly in bacteria, one found in eukaryotes/archaea (PheRS-A), and one localsied to eukaryotic mitochondria (PheRS-M). This page specifically refers to the α subunit of the bacterial variant (PheRS-Bα).

    Although both subunits of PheRS-B possess a class II catalytic domain, catalytic activity appears to be restricted to the α subunit. tRNA recognition and editing activity is restricted to β which is over twice the size of α (Fishman et al. 2001). The only other tetameric member of class II is the bacterial GlyRS, which is also composed of two catalytic α subunits and two non-catalytic β subunits. However unlike GlyRS, catalysis in PheRS is not attainable without the full tetrameric α2β2 assembly; monomers and dimers do not possess such activity (Mosyak et al. 1995). PheRS binds two tRNA molecules, where each tRNA binds across all four subunits (Goldgur et al. 1997).

    The C-terminal catalytic core of α is similar to that of the other PheRS familes, as well as SepRS and PylRS. Collectivey, these members constitute subclass IIc (Kavran et al. 2007, Perona et al. 2012, Valencia-Sánchez et al. 2016, Gomez et al. 2020).

    The catalytic domain of α-PheRS is similar to other class II members, and contains six antiparallel strands. Like most members of the superfamily, ATP binding is coordinated by the arginine tweezers, located in motifs 2 and 3 (Kaiser et al. 2018). The flexible N-terminal helical arm (A1) is intrinsically disordered, however it adopts a coiled coil structure upon interaction with the tRNA (Goldgur et al. 1997). Editing in the bacterial PheRS is performed by the β subunit.

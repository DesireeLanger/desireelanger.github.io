• The archaeal-like phenylalanyl-tRNA synthetase (PheRS-A) is an enzyme that plays a crucial role in protein synthesis by catalysing the attachment of the amino acid phenylalanine to its cognate tRNA:
    $ \text{Phe} + \text{tRNA}^\text{Phe} + \text{ATP} \xrightarrow{\text{PheRS-A}} \text{Phe-tRNA}^\text{Phe} + \text{AMP} + \text{PP}_i $
    The phenylalanyl-tRNA synthetases (PheRS) are considered to be one of the most evolutionarily complex enzymes among aminoacyl-tRNA synthetases (Klipcan et al. 2010). Unlike most members of the class II superfamily, which are homodimeric, PheRS-A operates as a heterotetramer composed of two α and two β subunits α2β2. There are three known variants of PheRS: one found predominantly in eukaryotes/archaea, one found in bacteria (PheRS-B), and one localsied to eukaryotic mitochondria (PheRS-M). This page specifically refers to the α subunit of the archaeal/eukaryotic variant (PheRS-Aα).

    Although both PheRS-A subunits possess a class II catalytic domain, catalytic activity appears to be restricted to the α subunit. tRNA recognition also occurs in the α chain (in contrast to the bacterial tetramer, for which tRNA binding occurs through the β chain), and editing activity is restricted to β. The C-terminal catalytic core of PheRS-Aα is similar to that of the other PheRS familes, as well as SepRS and PylRS. Collectivey, these members constitute subclass IIc (Kavran et al. 2007, Perona et al. 2012, Valencia-Sánchez et al. 2016, Gomez et al. 2020).

    The catalytic domain of PheRS-Aα is similar to other class II members, and contains six antiparallel strands. Like most members of the superfamily, ATP binding is coordinated by the arginine tweezers, located in motifs 2 and 3 (Kaiser et al. 2018). This families' catalytic domain is characterised by the short PheRS-Aα insertion module, which resides between motifs 2 and 3.

    The anticodon binding domain is proposed to be a short loop upstream of the catalytic core (Finarov et al. 2010). The N-terminal domains resemble DNA binding proteins, however their function roles are still under investigation (Ho et al. 2022).

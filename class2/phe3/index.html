• Phenylalanyl tRNA synthetase (PheRS) is an enzyme which attaches phenylalanine to its cognate tRNA:

    • Phe + tRNAPhe + ATP → Phe-tRNAPhe + AMP + PPi
    PheRS is probably the most complex of amino acyl tRNA synthetases (Klipcan et al. 2010). While most members of the class II superfamily are homodimeric, this enzyme operates as a heterotetramer α2β2. In total, there are three known variants of PheRS, and a total of five class II families (Finarov et al. 2010). Two of these variants are tetrameric - one predominantly found in bacteria (b-PheRS) and one in eukaryotes/archaea (e-PheRS) - while the other variant is monomeric - and found in mitochondria. This page details the α subunit of the eukaryotic variant. Also see the eukaryotic β subunit, the bacterial alpha subunit, the bacterial beta subunit, and the mitochondrial PheRS.

    Although both eukaryotic subunits possess a class II catalytic domain, catalytic activity appears to be restricted to the α subunit. tRNA recognition also occurs in the α chain (in contrast to the bacterial tetramer), while editing activity is restricted to β.

    The C-terminal catalytic core of α is similar to that of the other PheRS familes, as well as SepRS and PylRS. Collectivey, these members constitute subclass IIc (Kavran et al. 2007, Perona et al. 2012, Valencia-Sánchez et al. 2016, Gomez et al. 2020).

    The catalytic domain of α-PheRS is similar to other class II members, and contains six antiparallel strands. Like most members of the superfamily, ATP binding is coordinated by the arginine tweezers, located in motifs 2 and 3 (Kaiser et al. 2018). Much like the bacterial form, the sequence at the position of motif 1 is fairly distant to other class members.

    The anticodon binding domain is hypothesised to be a short loop upstream of the catalytic core (Finarov et al. 2010). The N-terminal domains resemble DNA binding proteins, however their function roles are still under investigation (Ho et al. 2022).

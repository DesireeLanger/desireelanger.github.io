• The Archael-like glycyl-tRNA synthetase (GlyRS-A) is a homodimeric enzyme that plays a crucial role in protein synthesis by catalysing the attachment of the amino acid glycine to its cognate tRNA:
    $ \text{Gly} + \text{tRNA}^\text{Gly} + \text{ATP} \xrightarrow{\text{GlyRS-A}} \text{Gly-tRNA}^\text{Gly} + \text{AMP} + \text{PP}_i $
    GlyRS-A belongs to the subclass IIa of aminoacyl tRNA synthetases, which also includes ProRS, SerRS, ThrRS, HisRS, and the eukaryotic-like GlyRS-E (Gomez et al. 2020, Valencia-Sánchez et al. 2016, Perona et al. 2012). The anticodon binding domains of subclass IIa, with the exception of SerRS, are homologous and are located at the C-terminal end (Wolf et al. 1999). The catalytic domains of GlyRS-A and GlyRS-E are characterized by the presence of two insertion modules, GlyRS-AE insertion modules 1 and 2, which are located between motifs 2 and 3. Insertion module 1 contains a short β strand which runs parallel with the six stranded anti-parallel fold. Insertion module 2 is absent in bacteria.

    The domain architecture of GlyRS-A is minimal, similar to HisRS and it appears to lack editing activity altogether (Gomez et al. 2020). GlyRS-A is present in most archaea and some bacteria, and is phylogenetically distinct from the tetrameric GlyRS, which is present in most bacteria (Shiba 2005).

Like most members of the class II aaRS superfamily, LysRS is homodimeric. LysRS catalyses the following reaction:
  • Lys + tRNALys + ATP → Lys-tRNALys + AMP + PPi
The three dimensional structure of LysRS closely resembles that of AsnRS and AspRS. Although the class II superfamily phylogeny is not entirely clear, there is good agreement that these three members are closely related, constituting subclass IIb (Cusack et al. 1991, Valencia-Sánchez et al. 2016, Cusack et al. 2020).

The N-terminal anticodon binding domain binds the second and third positions of the anticodon (Cusack et al. 1996). This enables binding specificty towards tRNALys, as opposed to tRNAAsn, as their anticodon binding domains are structurally similar, and their first two codon positions are identical in most genetic codes (AAA and AAG for Lys; AAC and AAT for Asn).

The C-terminal catalytic domain of LysRS is quite typical of a class II aaRS. Like most members of the superfamily, ATP binding is coordinated by the arginine tweezers, located in motifs 2 and 3 (Kaiser et al. 2018). The catalytic domain is a seven stranded antiparallel fold. In the active site, three metal ions bind to the β and γ phosphates of ATP (Ibba et al. 2005). Like many multimeric class II aaRS proteins, LysRS exhibits negative cooperativity, meaning that substrate binding in the actives site of one subunit lowers the catalytic activity of the other (Hughes et al. 2003). Editing in LysRS occurs at the pre-transfer level, despite lacking an edit domain. This process has been characterised for methionine, leucine, cysteine, alanine, threonine, homocysteine, homoserine, and ornithine (Jakubowski 1997, 1999).

Some archaea and bacteria instead have a class I LysRS - these two variants are from different superfamilies and are often presented as an example of convergent evolution. Some bacterial species, including E. coli, have two copies of the class II LysRS gene, with around 90% sequence identity (Ibba et al. 2005).

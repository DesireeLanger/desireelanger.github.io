• Histidyl-tRNA synthetase (HisRS) is an enzyme that plays a crucial role in protein synthesis by catalysing the attachment of the amino acid histidine to its cognate tRNA:
    $ \text{His} + \text{tRNA}^\text{His} + \text{ATP} \xrightarrow{\text{HisRS}} \text{His-tRNA}^\text{Thr} + \text{AMP} + \text{PP}_i $
    HisRS belongs to the subclass IIa, which includes other enzymes such as ProRS, SerRS, ThrRS, and the dimeric GlyRS (Gomez et al. 2020, Valencia-Sánchez et al. 2016, Perona et al. 2012). The anticodon binding domains of subclass IIa, with the exception of SerRS, are homologous and are located at the C-terminal end (Wolf et al. 1999). Unlike some other enzymes in this subclass, HisRS does not have editing activity (Gomez et al. 2020).

    The C-terminal catalytic domain of the archaeal HisRS is typical of a class II aminoacyl-tRNA synthetase. Like most members of the superfamily, ATP binding is coordinated by the arginine tweezers, located in motifs 2 and 3 (Kaiser et al. 2018). Studies have shown that even short enzyme constructs, around 130 amino acids, centered around motifs 1 and 2, can yield fast catalytic activity, suggesting that the regions downstream of motif 2 may have been later additions in early evolutionary history (Li et al. 2011). The catalytic domain is characterised by the ~100 residue HisRS insertion module which resides between motits 2 and 3 and contains a short β strand which runs parallel with the six stranded anti-parallel fold.

    In eukaryotes, HisRS is one of the few aminoacyl-tRNA synthetases which operate in both the cytoplasm as well as the mitochondria, alongside AlaRS, GlyRS-E, and ValRS. Localisation into these compartments is achieved through alternative initiation, which governs the expression of an N-terminal mitochondrial localisation signal (Natsoulis et al. 1986, Chiu et al. 1992).

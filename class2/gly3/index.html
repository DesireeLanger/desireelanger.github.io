• The Eukaryote-like glycyl-tRNA synthetase (GlyRS-E) is a homodimeric enzyme that plays a crucial role in protein synthesis by catalysing the attachment of the amino acid glycine to its cognate tRNA:
    $ \text{Gly} + \text{tRNA}^\text{Gly} + \text{ATP} \xrightarrow{\text{GlyRS-E}} \text{Gly-tRNA}^\text{Gly} + \text{AMP} + \text{PP}_i $
    GlyRS-E belongs to the subclass IIa of aminoacyl tRNA synthetases, which also includes ProRS, SerRS, ThrRS, HisRS, and the archaeal-like GlyRS-A (Gomez et al., 2020; Valencia-Sánchez et al., 2016; Perona et al., 2012). The anticodon binding domains of subclass IIa, with the exception of SerRS, are homologous and are located at the C-terminal end (Wolf et al., 1999). A serine-to-leucine mutation in the anticodon binding domain of the human GlyRS-E impairs mitochindrial metabolism in neurons, and is implicated in motor nerve degeneration (Cader et al. 2007, Boczonadi et al., 2018). The domain architecture of GlyRS-E is minimal, similar to HisRS, and it appears to lack editing activity altogether (Gomez et al., 2020).

    The catalytic domains of GlyRS-A and GlyRS-E are characterised by the presence of two insertion modules, GlyRS-AE insertion modules 1 and 2, which are located between motifs 2 and 3. Insertion module 1 contains a short β strand which runs parallel with the six stranded anti-parallel fold. Insertion module 2 is present in most eukaryotic and archaeal GlyRS enzymes, but is absent in bacteria. This insert often contains a lysine rich region, which may be involved in differentiating between cytoplasmic and mitochondrial tRNA Gly acceptor stems (Chen et al. 2012).

    GlyRS-E is one of the few aminoacyl-tRNA synthetases which operate in both the cytoplasm as well as the mitochondria, alongside AlaRS, HisRS, and ValRS. Localisation into these compartments is achieved through alternative initiation, which governs the expression of an N-terminal mitochondrial localisation signal (Shiba et al. 1994, Chang et al. 2004). Some eukaryotes contain an additional GlyRS-E genes known as GRS2, which appears to be silent under normal conditions in S. cerevisiae and is lacking the lysine rich insert (Chen et al. 2012).

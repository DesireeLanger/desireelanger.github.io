• The Eukaryote-like glycyl-tRNA synthetase (GlyRS-E) is a homodimeric enzyme that plays a crucial role in protein synthesis by catalysing the attachment of the amino acid glycine to its cognate tRNA:
    $ \text{Gly} + \text{tRNA}^\text{Gly} + \text{ATP} \xrightarrow{\text{GlyRS-E}} \text{Gly-tRNA}^\text{Gly} + \text{AMP} + \text{PP}_i $
    GlyRS-E belongs to the subclass IIa of aminoacyl tRNA synthetases, which also includes ProRS, SerRS, ThrRS, HisRS, and the archaeal-like GlyRS-E (Gomez et al., 2020; Valencia-Sánchez et al., 2016; Perona et al., 2012). The anticodon binding domains of subclass IIa, with the exception of SerRS, are homologous and are located at the C-terminal end (Wolf et al., 1999). The catalytic domains of GlyRS-A and GlyRS-E are characterised by the presence of two insertion modules, GlyRS-AE insertion modules 1 and 2, which are located between motifs 2 and 3. Insertion module 1 contains a short β strand which runs parallel with the six stranded anti-parallel fold. Insertion module 2 contains a lysine rich insert in GlyRS-E, which is absent from GlyRS-A. The lysine rich insertion may be involved in differentiating between cytoplasmic and mitochondrial tRNA Gly acceptor stems (Chen et al. 2012). The entire module is absent in bacteria.

    The domain architecture of GlyRS-E is minimal, similar to HisRS and it appears to lack editing activity altogether (Gomez et al., 2020). GlyRS-E is present in eukaryotes and it stands apart from other aminoacyl tRNA synthetases because it is involved in translation in both the cytoplasm as well as the mitochondria, unlike most aminoacyl-tRNA synthetases which are localised into one of the two compartments (Boczonadi et al., 2018).

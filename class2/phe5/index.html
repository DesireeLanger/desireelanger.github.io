• The mitochondrial phenylalanyl tRNA synthetase (m-PheRS) is an enzyme which attaches phenylalanine to its cognate tRNA:

    • Phe + tRNAPhe + ATP → Phe-tRNAPhe + AMP + PPi
    Although it resides in the mitochondria, the gene which encodes m-PheRS is nuclear.

    PheRS is probably the most complex of amino acyl tRNA synthetases (Klipcan et al. 2010). There are three variants of PheRS. This variant is the only monomeric member of the class II superfamily. The other two variants are heterotetrameric - one predominantly found in bacteria, and the other in eukaryotes/archaea (Finarov et al. 2010). As the two variants are heterotetrameric, they are comprised of four families: the bacterial α subunit, the bacterial β subunit, the eukaryotic α subunit, and the eukaryotic β subunit.

    The catalytic core is similar to that of the other PheRS familes, as well as SepRS and PylRS. Collectivey, these members constitute subclass IIc (Kavran et al. 2007, Perona et al. 2012, Valencia-Sánchez et al. 2016, Gomez et al. 2020). Like most members of the superfamily, ATP binding is coordinated by the arginine tweezers, located in motifs 2 and 3 (Kaiser et al. 2018). The core is most similar to the α bacterial subunit of PheRS, and much like this paralog, the sequence at the position of motif 1 is fairly distant to other class members. In contrast, the C-terminal anticodon binding domain appears to be borrowed from the bacterial β subunit (domain B8; Klipcan et al. 2010). As such, the mitochondrial PheRS is likely a chimera between the two bacterial PheRS subunits.

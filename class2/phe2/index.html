• Phenylalanyl tRNA synthetase (PheRS) is an enzyme which attaches phenylalanine to its cognate tRNA:

    • Phe + tRNAPhe + ATP → Phe-tRNAPhe + AMP + PPi
    PheRS is probably the most complex of amino acyl tRNA synthetases (Klipcan et al. 2010). While most members of the class II superfamily are homodimeric, this enzyme operates as a heterotetramer α2β2. In total, there are three known variants of PheRS, and a total of five class II families (Finarov et al. 2010). Two of these variants are heterotetrameric - one predominantly found in bacteria (b-PheRS) and one in eukaryotes/archaea (e-PheRS) - while the other variant is monomeric - and found in mitochondria. This page details the β subunit of the bacterial variant. Also see the bacterial α subunit, the eukaryotic α subunit, the eukaryotic β subunit, and the mitochondrial PheRS.

    Although both bacterial subunits possess a class II catalytic domain, catalytic activity appears to be restricted to the α subunit. tRNA recognition and editing activity is restricted to β which is over twice the size of α (Fishman et al. 2001). The only other tetameric member of class II is the bacterial GlyRS, which is also composed of two catalytic α subunits and two non-catalytic β subunits. However unlike GlyRS, catalysis in PheRS is not attainable without the full tetrameric α2β2 assembly; monomers and dimers do not possess such activity (Mosyak et al. 1995). PheRS binds two tRNA molecules, where each tRNA binds across all four subunits (Goldgur et al. 1997).

    The C-terminal catalytic core of β is similar to that of the other PheRS familes, as well as SepRS and PylRS. Collectivey, these members constitute subclass IIc (Kavran et al. 2007, Perona et al. 2012, Valencia-Sánchez et al. 2016, Gomez et al. 2020).

    The catalytic domain does not appear to possess catalytic activity (Fishman et al. 2001), but still contains the six antiparallel strands characteristic of a class II aaRS. As such, the sequence at the stuctural positions of motifs 1, 2, and 3 do not follow the motif patterns. The positions one would normally expect to see these motifs are labelled for convenience.

    The β-PheRS editing domain facilitates the removal of mischarged tyrosyl groups from tRNAPhe. The editing domain is located at domains B3-B4, near the N-terminus (Roy et al. 2004, Mosyak et al. 1995). The anticodon binding domain (domain B8) is located at the C-terminus. This domain is unique in its charging of the 2′OH of tRNA, while other members of class II charge 3′OH (Goldgur et al. 1997). This contrasts with the eukaryotic PheRS tetramer, whose anticodon binding domain resides in the α chain.

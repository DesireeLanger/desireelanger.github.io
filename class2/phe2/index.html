• The bacterial-like phenylalanyl-tRNA synthetase (PheRS-B) is an enzyme that plays a crucial role in protein synthesis by catalysing the attachment of the amino acid phenylalanine to its cognate tRNA:
    $ \text{Phe} + \text{tRNA}^\text{Phe} + \text{ATP} \xrightarrow{\text{PheRS-B}} \text{Phe-tRNA}^\text{Phe} + \text{AMP} + \text{PP}_i $
    The phenylalanyl-tRNA synthetases (PheRS) are considered to be one of the most evolutionarily complex enzymes among aminoacyl-tRNA synthetases (Klipcan et al. 2010). Unlike most members of the class II superfamily, which are homodimeric, PheRS-B operates as a heterotetramer composed of two α and two β subunits α2β2. There are three known variants of PheRS: one found predominantly in bacteria, one found in eukaryotes/archaea (PheRS-A), and one localsied to eukaryotic mitochondria (PheRS-M). This page specifically refers to the β subunit of the bacterial variant (PheRS-Bβ).

    The catalytic activity of PheRS-B is restricted to the α subunit, while tRNA recognition and editing activity is restricted to the β subunit, which is over twice the size of the α subunit (Fishman et al. 2001). The only other tetrameric member of class II is the bacterial GlyRS, which is also composed of two catalytic α subunits and two non-catalytic β subunits. However, unlike GlyRS, catalysis in PheRS-B is not possible without the full tetrameric assembly (Mosyak et al. 1995). PheRS-B binds two tRNA molecules, with each tRNA binding across all four subunits (Goldgur et al. 1997).

    The C-terminal catalytic core of the β subunit is similar to that of other PheRS families, as well as SepRS, and belongs to subclass IIc (Kavran et al. 2007, Perona et al. 2012, Valencia-Sánchez et al. 2016, Gomez et al. 2020). The catalytic domain does not possess catalytic activity (Fishman et al. 2001), but still contains the six antiparallel strands characteristic of a class II aaRS. As such, the sequence at the stuctural positions of motifs 1, 2, and 3 do not follow the motif pattern, but the positions one would normally expect to see these motifs are labelled for convenience.

    The β subunit's editing domain facilitates the removal of mischarged tyrosyl groups from tRNAPhe. The editing domain is located at domains B3-B4, near the N-terminus (Roy et al. 2004, Mosyak et al. 1995). The anticodon binding domain (domain B8) is located at the C-terminus. This domain is unique in its charging of the 2′OH of tRNA, while other members of class II charge 3′OH (Goldgur et al. 1997). This contrasts with the eukaryotic PheRS tetramer, whose anticodon binding domain resides in the α chain. A paralog of the N-terminal RNA binding domain EMAP is also found in some forms of TyrRS and MetRS (Wolf et al. 1999).

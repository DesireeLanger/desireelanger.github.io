• The tetrameric GlyRS catalyses the following reaction:
    • Gly + tRNAGly + ATP → Gly-tRNAGly + AMP + PPi
    While most members of the class II superfamily are homodimeric, this enzyme operates as a heterotetramer α2β2. This page details the α subunit; the β subunit does not contain a catalytic domain. The only other tetameric member of class II is PheRS, which is composed of two catalytic α subunits and two catalytic β subunits.

    The catalytic core of t-GlyRS is similar to AlaRS. Although the class II superfamily phylogeny is not entirely clear, these two members appear to be closely related, constituting subclass IIc or IId depending on the author (Valencia-Sánchez et al. 2016, Gomez et al. 2020).

    Both subunit species are indispensible for GlyRS activity (Ostrem et al. 1974). The α subunits contains all of the determinants for activating glycine, and are able to do so as a dimer without β (Valencia-Sánchez et al. 2016). This dimerisation is supported by a C-terminal three helix bundle (Tan et al. 2012). The β subunits are significantly larger in size (65 kDa, as opposed to 35 kDa) and contain tRNA recognition elements essential for producing Gly-tRNAGly (Nagel et al. 1984).

    The catalytic domain of t-GlyRS is significantly smaller than other class II members, and contains six antiparallel strands rather than seven. Like most members of the superfamily, ATP binding is coordinated by the arginine tweezers, located in motifs 2 and 3 (Kaiser et al. 2018).

    It appears that t-GlyRS lacks editing activity (Gomez et al. 2020). t-GlyRS is present in most bacteria and is phylogenetically distinct from the dimeric GlyRS, which is present in archaea, eukaryotes, and some bacteria (Ibba et al. 2005).

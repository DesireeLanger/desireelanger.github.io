• The Archaeal-like Seryl-tRNA synthetase (SerRS-A) is an enzyme that plays a crucial role in protein synthesis by catalysing the attachment of the amino acid serine to its cognate tRNA:
    $ \text{Ser} + \text{tRNA}^\text{Ser} + \text{ATP} \xrightarrow{\text{SerRS-A}} \text{Ser-tRNA}^\text{Ser} + \text{AMP} + \text{PP}_i $
    SerRS-A is atypical, and is found in methanogenic archaea. It is evolutionarily distinct from the standard SerRS, which is found in most organisms. The archaeal Methanosarcina barkeri genome encodes for both variants. This enzyme has not been characterised as extensively as other amino acyl tRNA synthetases.

    The C-terminal catalytic domain SerRS-A is quite typical of a class II aaRS. Like most members of the superfamily, ATP binding is coordinated by the arginine tweezers, located in motifs 2 and 3 (Kaiser et al. 2018). The catalytic domain is characterised by the ~35 residue SerRS-A insertion module located between motifs 1 and 2.

    The two SerRS paralogs differ primarily in their N-terminal tRNA binding domain. However, they also demonstrate distinct modes of serine binding via the catalytic domain, with the archaeal SerRS employing a tetra-coordinated zinc ion to coordinate serine binding at the active site (Bilokapic et al. 2006). Unlike the standard SerRS, it appears that the archaeal SerRS lacks editing activity (Bilokapic et al. 2006).

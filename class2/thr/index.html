• Threonyl-tRNA synthetase (ThrRS) is an enzyme that plays a crucial role in protein synthesis by catalysing the attachment of the amino acid threonine to its cognate tRNA:
    $ \text{Thr} + \text{tRNA}^\text{Thr} + \text{ATP} \xrightarrow{\text{ThrRS}} \text{Thr-tRNA}^\text{Thr} + \text{AMP} + \text{PP}_i $
    ThrRS belongs to the subclass IIa, which includes other enzymes such as HisRS, ProRS, SerRS, and the dimeric GlyRS (Gomez et al. 2020, Valencia-Sánchez et al. 2016, Perona et al. 2012). The anticodon binding domains of subclass IIa, with the exception of SerRS, are homologous and are located at the C-terminal end (Wolf et al. 1999). The catalytic domain of ThrRS is typical of a class II aminoacyl-tRNA synthetase. Like most members of the superfamily, ATP binding is coordinated by the arginine tweezers, located in motifs 2 and 3 (Kaiser et al. 2018). The catalytic domain is characterised by the ~40 residue ThrRS insertion module which resides between motits 2 and 3 and contains a short β strand which runs parallel with the six stranded anti-parallel fold.

    ThrRS also has an editing function, which occurs at the post-transfer level to remove mischarged serines from tRNAThr (Dock-Bregeon et al. 2000). This is achieved through the two N-terminal domains N1 and N2 (Dock-Bregeon et a. 2004). In some archaea, a non-homologous N-terminal editing domain, which also targets mischarged serine, is present instead of the N1 and N2 domains (Hussain et al. 2006). The editing domain of ThrRS is located upstream of the catalytic domain and is homologous with that of AlaRS, which is conversely located downstream of the catalytic domain (Sankaranarayanan et al. 1999). This highlights the complex evolutionary history of aminoacyl-tRNA synthetases and the diversity of structural arrangements observed among these enzymes.

• O-phosphoseryl-tRNA synthetase (SepRS) is an enzyme that plays a crucial role in protein synthesis by catalysing the attachment of the non-proteogenic amino acid o-phosphoserine to the tRNA of cysteine:
    $ \text{Sep} + \text{tRNA}^\text{Cys} + \text{ATP} \xrightarrow{\text{SepRS}} \text{Sep-tRNA}^\text{Cys} + \text{AMP} + \text{PP}_i $
    In certain methanogenic archaea, the standard class I CysRS enzyme is absent, thus, the non-canonical SepRS charges tRNACys with o-phosphoserine, which is then converted into cysteine by Cys-tRNA synthetase (Sauerwald et al. 2005). Unlike most members of the class II superfamily, SepRS exists as a homotetramer (Fukunaga and Shigeyuki 2007).

    The catalytic domain of SepRS shares structural similarities with that of PylRS and the five PheRS families, which collectively constitute subclass IIc (Kavran et al. 2007, Perona et al. 2012, Valencia-Sánchez et al. 2016, Gomez et al. 2020). The catalytic domain is typical among class II enzymes, containing six antiparallel strands. The binding of ATP is coordinated by the arginine tweezers, located in motifs 2 and 3 (Kaiser et al. 2018). The catalytic domain is characterized by the 70-80 residue SepRS insertion module, which resides between motifs 1 and 2. The C-terminal domain binds to the anticodon of tRNACys (Fukunaga and Shigeyuki 2007). It has been reported that SepRS lacks editing activity (Gomez et al. 2020).

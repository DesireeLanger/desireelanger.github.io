• Phenylalanyl tRNA synthetase (PheRS) is an enzyme which attaches phenylalanine to its cognate tRNA:

    • Phe + tRNAPhe + ATP → Phe-tRNAPhe + AMP + PPi
    PheRS is probably the most complex of amino acyl tRNA synthetases (Klipcan et al. 2010). While most members of the class II superfamily are homodimeric, this enzyme operates as a heterotetramer α2β2. In total, there are three known variants of PheRS, and a total of five class II families (Finarov et al. 2010). Two of these variants are heterotetrameric - one predominantly found in bacteria (b-PheRS) and one in eukaryotes/archaea (e-PheRS) - while the other variant is monomeric - and found in mitochondria. This page details the β subunit of the eukaryotic variant. Also see the eukaryotic α subunit, the bacterial α subunit, the bacterial β subunit, and the mitochondrial PheRS.

    The C-terminal catalytic core of β is similar to that of the other PheRS familes, as well as SepRS and PylRS. Collectivey, these members constitute subclass IIc (Kavran et al. 2007, Perona et al. 2012, Valencia-Sánchez et al. 2016, Gomez et al. 2020).

    Although both eukaryotic subunits possess a class II catalytic domain, catalytic activity appears to be restricted to the α subunit. tRNA recognition also occurs in the α chain (in contrast to the bacterial tetramer), while editing activity is restricted to β.

    The catalytic domain does not appear to possess catalytic activity (Fishman et al. 2001), but still contains the six antiparallel strands characteristic of a class II aaRS. As such, the sequence at the stuctural positions of motifs 1, 2, and 3 do not follow the motif patterns. The positions one would normally expect to see these motifs are labelled for convenience.

    The editing domain (hB3 and hB4) is located near the N-terminal and resembles that of the bacterial β PheRS subunit (Finarov et al. 2010), which is known to target mischarged tyrosines (Roy et al. 2004).

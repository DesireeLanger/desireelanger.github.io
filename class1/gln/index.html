• Glutaminyl-tRNA synthetase (GlnRS) is an enzyme that plays a crucial role in protein synthesis by catalysing the attachment of the amino acid glutamine to its cognate tRNA:
    $ \text{Gln} + \text{tRNA}^\text{Gln} + \text{ATP} \xrightarrow{\text{GlnRS}} \text{Gln-tRNA}^\text{Gln} + \text{AMP} + \text{PP}_i $
    Many organisms, including archaea and some bacteria, do not possess GlnRS, in which case the incorporation of glutamine into the polypeptide can be accomplished through the use of a non-discriminating GlxRS coupled with amidotransferase activity (Lapointe et al. 1986, Raczniak et al. 2001). The same mechanism is employed by the Class II AspRS and AsnRS families, leading to the hypothesis that AsnRS and GlnRS were among the last of the synthetases to evolve (de Pouplana and Schimmel, 2001, Hadd et al. 2014).

    The N-terminal catalytic domain of GlnRS closely resembles that of GluRS and GlxRS, constituting subclass Ib (Perona et al. 2012, Gomez et al., 2020). Their catalytic domains are characterised by an insertion within CP1, containing a loop flanked by two helices (SC1b IM), which may play a role in acceptor stem recognition (Rath et al. 1998, Nureki et al. 2010). Its anticodon binding domain is located at the C-terminal (Rould et al. 1991) and is homologous with that of GlxRS but not GluRS.

    In many eukaryotes, the N-terminal contains a helical domain homologous to the Yqey domain found in amidotransferases (Hadd et al. 2014), and is required for the assembly of the MARS complex (Kim et al. 2000). It may also contribute to the enzyme's ability to discriminate against tRNAGlu (Hadd et al. 2014).

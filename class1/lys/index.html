• The class I lysyl-tRNA synthetase (LysRS-I) is a monomeric that plays a crucial role in protein synthesis by catalysing the attachment of the amino acid arginine to its cognate tRNA:
    $ \text{Lys} + \text{tRNA}^\text{Lys} + \text{Lys} \xrightarrow{\text{LysRS-I}} \text{Lys-tRNA}^\text{Lys} + \text{AMP} + \text{PP}_i $
    The C-terminal anticodon binding domain is α-helical rich and is very similar to that of GluRS (Terada et al. 2002), but quite distinct from the other two members of the subclass. However, the catalytic domain is not immediately related to any other aminoacyl-tRNA syntheatses, and as such, the enzyme does not belong to a subclass.

    The N-terminal catalytic domain of LysRS-I is quite typical of a class I aaRS. Like most members of the superfamily, ATP binding is coordinated by the backbone brackets (Kaiser et al. 2018). The catalytic domain is the standard four stranded Rossman fold, with the additional fifth strand found in some members of the family. Editing activity has not been characteristed for LysRS-I (Gomez and Ibba 2020). The CP1 domain conists of a four stranded anti-parellel β-sheet and two α helices (Terada et al. 2002). It resides between the HIGH and KMSKS motifs of the catalytic domain, and is found in subclasses Ia and Ib (Sugiura et al. 2000). Its phylogeny appears to be quite distinct from the flanking urzyme, suggesting distinct origins for the two regions (Carter Jr et al. 2022).

    The class I LysRS is present in most archaea and some bacteria. Whereas, eukaryotes and most bacteria instead have a class II LysRS. These two variants are from different superfamilies and are often presented as an example of convergent evolution.
